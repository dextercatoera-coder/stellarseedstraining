Section 01

Introduction & Importance of Proper Drying

Why mechanical drying is essential for preserving seed quality from harvest through storage.

What We Want: High Quality Seed

The ultimate goal of seed corn drying is to produce and preserve high quality seed with:

  • Good warm germination
  • Good seed vigor
  • Resulting in good field emergence

Maximum Quality Potential

Maximum quality potential is reached at physiological maturity. From that point forward, quality must be preserved through careful handling at every stage.

How Quality Is Preserved

Seed quality needs to be preserved through four critical practices:

  • Harvesting as close as possible to physiological maturity
  • Good drying practices
  • Good conditioning practices
  • Good storage practices
Key Takeaway

Mechanical drying is necessary to preserve seed quality. Without it, seed deteriorates rapidly after physiological maturity.

Section 02

How Does Ear Corn & Kernels Dry?

Understanding moisture movement from the center of the kernel to the surrounding atmosphere.

Moisture Movement in Ear Corn

  • Moisture on the outside surface moves into the surrounding atmosphere
  • Internal moisture moves past or through the kernels to the outside surface

How a Kernel Dries

Water evaporates from the surface of the kernel. As this happens, water moves from the center of the kernel to the surface.

Seeds Are Hygroscopic

  • After physiological maturity, seed moisture is a function of the surrounding air
  • Seed moisture will be determined by relative humidity and temperature of the air
  • Equilibrium seed moisture content is a function of water vapor pressure
  • These principles are used to dry seed in the dryers
What does "hygroscopic" mean?

Seeds naturally absorb or release moisture based on surrounding air conditions. This property is the entire basis for mechanical seed drying — by controlling the air, we control seed moisture.

Section 03

Drying Rate & Determining Factors

What controls how fast seed dries and how to express and manage the drying rate.

What Is Drying Rate?

Drying rate is expressed as the length of time utilized in removing a certain amount of water, measured in number of hours per point of moisture removed.

Example: Drying from 35% to 12% moisture in 69 hours
Drying Rate = 69 / (35 − 12) = 3.0 hours per point

Quality Plan Drying Rate Goals

Sensitivity LevelHours Per Point
Least Sensitive3.0
Moderately Sensitive3.5
Sensitive4.0

Factors Determining Drying Rate

  • Points of seed moisture removed — Higher initial moisture releases moisture easier; it takes longer at low moisture
  • Air temperature — Higher temperatures accelerate drying rate
  • Air relative humidity — Lower RH accelerates drying rate
  • Airflow rate — Faster airflow accelerates drying rate
  • Ability of product to release moisture — Hybrids differ due to seed morphology differences

Effect of Temperature on Drying Rate

Seed dries slower at 35°C compared to 43°C. Drying rate increases with higher temperature.

At 35°C: HRPT = 21.48 − 0.7130 × HARMST + 0.007278 × HARMST²

At 43°C: HRPT = 7.542 − 0.1854 × HARMST + 0.001539 × HARMST²
Critical Warning

High drying rates, especially at high seed moisture content, can damage seed. Potential drying rate is faster with higher initial seed moisture, but this does not mean it should be used aggressively.

Section 04

Air Temperature & Relative Humidity

Understanding how air properties drive the drying process.

Air Temperature

  • Inlet Temperature (dry bulb temperature): The higher the temperature, the faster the drying rate
  • Down air — not to exceed 110°F (43°C)
  • Up air — not to exceed 100°F (38°C)

Relative Humidity

Relative humidity is the ratio of the amount of water vapor in the air to the amount of water vapor when saturated at the same temperature, expressed in percentage (%).

Fundamental Principle

The potential of air holding water increases with an increase in temperature.

What Happens When Inlet Temperature Increases

  • Water molecules in corn kernels get energized
  • Relative humidity decreases

When both happen, water moves from inside the kernels into the air.

Psychrometric Example

If the temperature increases from 25°C to 35°C at 50% RH, the RH will decrease to 28% at sea level.

Temperature & RH Targets (Double Pass Reversing Dryers)

Temperate Conditions

ParameterTarget
Lower Tunnel RH25 – 30%
Upper Tunnel RH15 – 20%
Lower Tunnel TempMin 35°C/95°F; Max per quality manual
Upper Tunnel TempMin to meet lower tunnel temp; Max 43°C/110°F

Tropical Conditions

  • Adjust temperatures to reflect ambient air conditions (wet bulb)
  • If RH is low, temperature should be reduced
  • If RH is high, temperature may need to be increased
  • For every degree of wet bulb above 25°C/77°F, dry bulb can increase by 1°C/2°F — up to max 40°C/104°F (up air) and 46°C/115°F (down air)

Section 06

Airflow Rate & The Drying Front

How airflow affects drying patterns and overall uniformity.

Airflow Rate

Expressed as cubic meters of air per minute per cubic meter of corn.

  • Higher airflow = faster drying — up to a point
  • At lower seed moisture, the effect becomes less influential
  • Minimum: 10 m³/m³ ear corn/min

The Drying Front

  • The drying front is the leading edge of the drying zone
  • It advances in the direction of air flow
  • Greater airflow = faster front displacement & deeper drying zone
  • Slow displacement and incomplete overlapping of drying zones create variability in seed moisture

Assessing Drying Rates

  • During drying — to manage the process
  • Post-mortem (immediately after a bin is done) — to adjust future bins
  • After the drying season — to evaluate efficiency

What to Look For

  • Displacement of exhaust temperature from wet bulb temperature line
  • Amount of moisture removed
  • Total hours of drying time
  • Up air and down air inlet temperatures
  • Fill depth
  • Wet bulb temperature (starting point)
  • Air pressure
  • Drying sensitivity rating

Section 07

Rules for Avoiding Drying Injury & Summary

Practical rules to protect seed quality and comprehensive module summary.

Rules for Avoiding Drying Injury

  • Follow recommended drying rates and temperatures for a hybrid
  • Avoid filling dryer bins too full (> 3 m)
  • Avoid shallow bins (< 1 m)
  • Adjust dryer conditions as ambient air conditions change
  • When in doubt, seek advice
When Is Seed Quality at Greatest Risk?

Seed quality is at greatest risk at the start of drying when seed moisture is still high. Quality is also at risk when airflow is reversed too soon and the drying front has not moved completely through the seed.

Module Summary

  • Mechanical drying is necessary to preserve seed quality
  • Seeds are hygroscopic — the basis for seed drying principles
  • Drying rate is mainly influenced by: seed moisture, air temperature, relative humidity, and air flow rate
  • The drying front and zone must move across the whole seed bed
  • An aggressive drying rate can cause seed injury; a slow rate reduces throughput and can also contribute to poor quality
