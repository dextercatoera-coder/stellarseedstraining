Section 01

Introduction & Importance of Proper Drying

Why mechanical drying is essential for preserving seed quality from harvest through storage.

What We Want: High Quality Seed

The ultimate goal of seed corn drying is to produce and preserve high quality seed with:

  • Good warm germination
  • Good seed vigor
  • Resulting in good field emergence

Maximum Quality Potential

Maximum quality potential is reached at physiological maturity. From that point forward, quality must be preserved through careful handling at every stage.

How Quality Is Preserved

Seed quality needs to be preserved through four critical practices:

  • Harvesting as close as possible to physiological maturity
  • Good drying practices
  • Good conditioning practices
  • Good storage practices
Key Takeaway

Mechanical drying is necessary to preserve seed quality.

Section 02

How Does Ear Corn & Kernels Dry?

Understanding moisture movement from the kernel center to the atmosphere.

Moisture Movement in Ear Corn

  • Moisture on the outside surface moves into the surrounding atmosphere
  • Internal moisture moves past or through the kernels to the outside surface

How a Kernel Dries

into Air

Water evaporates from the surface. As this happens, water moves from the center to the surface.

Seeds Are Hygroscopic

  • After physiological maturity, seed moisture is a function of the surrounding air
  • Seed moisture will be determined by relative humidity and temperature of the air
  • Equilibrium seed moisture content is a function of water vapor pressure
  • These principles are used to dry seed in the dryers

Section 03

Drying Rate & Determining Factors

What Is Drying Rate?

Drying rate is expressed as the length of time utilized in removing a certain amount of water, measured in number of hours per point of moisture removed.

Example: 69 hours / (35 − 12) = 3.0 hours per point

Factors Determining Drying Rate

  • Points of seed moisture removed
  • Air temperature — higher temperatures accelerate drying
  • Air relative humidity — lower RH accelerates drying
  • Airflow rate — faster airflow accelerates drying
  • Ability of product to release moisture
Critical Warning

High drying rates at high seed moisture can damage seed.

Section 04

Air Temperature & Relative Humidity

Air Temperature Limits

  • Down air — not to exceed 110°F, or 43°C
  • Up air — not to exceed 100°F, or 38°C

What Happens When Temperature Increases

  • Water molecules in corn kernels get energized
  • Relative humidity decreases

When both happen, water moves from inside the kernels into the air.

Section 05

Quality Plan Guidelines & Reversal Decisions

Reverse Air / Shelling Decisions

When to Reverse Air:

  • More than 50% of total drying cycle completed
  • Safe to reverse at 25% corn moisture or less

When to Start Shelling:

  • Target of 12.5% corn kernel moisture
  • Consult the local quality plan

Section 06

Airflow Rate & The Drying Front

Airflow Rate

  • Higher airflow equals faster drying, up to a point
  • At lower seed moisture, the effect becomes less influential
  • Minimum requirement is 10 cubic meters per cubic meter of ear corn per minute

The Drying Front

  • The drying front is the leading edge of the drying zone
  • It advances in the direction of air flow
  • Greater airflow means faster front displacement and deeper drying zone
  • Slow displacement and incomplete overlapping create variability in seed moisture

Section 07

Rules for Avoiding Drying Injury & Summary

Rules for Avoiding Drying Injury

  • Follow recommended drying rates and temperatures for a hybrid
  • Avoid filling dryer bins too full, more than 3 meters
  • Avoid shallow bins, less than 1 meter
  • Adjust dryer conditions as ambient air conditions change
  • When in doubt about dryer management, seek advice
Greatest Risk

Seed quality is at greatest risk at the start of drying when seed moisture is still high, and when airflow is reversed too soon.
